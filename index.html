Context

Huntech is a New Zealand outdoor retailer specialising in technical hunting gear and equipment.

However, its eCommerce experience was no longer keeping up with the needs of the business.

The platform had several limitations that were affecting both the shopping experience and conversion opportunities.

My role was to lead the UI redesign, working within a multidisciplinary team under our Design Lead.

Problem

To understand where the biggest opportunities were, I conducted a heuristic analysis of the entire website.

I reviewed the customer journey looking for friction points that could affect the user experience and business goals.

We prioritised the most important areas based on their impact on conversion.

As a result, we focused on mobile, particularly on the Product Listing Page (PLP) and Product Detail Page (PDP).

Solution

I translated those findings into practical UI solutions that helped users make purchase decisions with more confidence.

Problem → Solution → Impact

The first opportunity was around Size and Stock.

Users could see out-of-stock products, size selection was inconsistent, and the Size Guide had low visibility in the purchase flow.

To solve this, I introduced real-time stock visibility, standardised sizing patterns, and a more integrated and visible Size Guide directly on the product page.

This helped users see availability earlier and make purchase decisions with more confidence.

The second opportunity was around Navigation.

The mobile navigation didn’t follow a standard pattern, the search experience had no guidance, and the category structure was too text-heavy.

To solve this, I introduced a more intuitive navigation layout, guided search with Best Sellers, and visual category tiles.

This helped users discover products faster and reduce cognitive load.

The third opportunity was around Mobile Filtering.

Filtering was fragmented across multiple steps, some touch targets were too small on mobile, and there was no sorting option.

To solve this, I combined filtering and sorting into the same layout and introduced 48px touch-safe targets.

This made filtering easier to use with one hand and helped users find products faster.

The fourth opportunity was around Product Information and Calls to Action.

Technical benefits were only explained through text, and users could lose the Add to Cart button while scrolling.

To solve this, I introduced visual icons to highlight key product benefits and a sticky Add to Cart button.

This made product information easier to understand and reduced friction during the purchase process.

The last opportunity was around Product Merchandising.

Users had to open the product page before adding an item to the cart, and product cards were inconsistent across the experience.

To solve this, I introduced a quick-add option directly from the listing page and made product cards more consistent across the site.

This reduced unnecessary steps and created a more consistent shopping experience.

Design System & Delivery

I also worked within the Design System, adding new components, improving existing ones, and preparing the final designs for development handoff in Figma.

Result

After launch, the agency reported a 125% increase in revenue and a 41% increase in AOV (Average Order Value).

Revenue is the total sales generated by the business, while AOV measures the average value of each order.

While many factors can influence business performance, these results showed that improving the user experience and reducing friction can have a significant impact on commercial outcomes.

Why I'm Proud

I'm proud of this project because it showed how effective UI decisions can improve both the user experience and business performance.

It was a great example of how design, business goals, and technology can work together to create measurable results.

For me, CRO is about finding and removing friction points in the user journey to help users complete their goals and improve business outcomes, such as conversion rate or sales.

My approach starts with identifying friction points through techniques like heuristic analysis, analytics, heatmaps, session recordings, or A/B testing.

Then, I focus on three key areas.

First, Conversion Rate, by making the core journey simple and easy to complete.

Second, Cart Abandonment, by removing the reasons why users drop off during the purchase journey.

Finally, Average Order Value, by finding opportunities to add value to each order through upselling or cross-selling.

For example, in my Huntech project, I used heuristic analysis to identify friction points on mobile.

After launch, the agency reported a 125% increase in revenue and a 41% increase in Average Order Value year-on-year.

1. CR / CVR (Conversion Rate)

Es la métrica principal. Si la web no convierte, ninguna otra métrica importa. Todo tu trabajo de UX/UI impacta directamente acá.

2. Cart Abandonment Rate

Es el cuello de botella más crítico. El usuario ya mostró intención de compra (metió algo al carrito) pero se trabó al final. Tu foco en el diseño del checkout es bajar este porcentaje.

3. AOV (Average Order Value)

Una vez que lográs que la web convierta de forma estable (Paso 1 y 2), el siguiente paso del negocio es que cada cliente gaste más plata en esa compra a través de componentes de Upselling o Cross-selling.

En resumen: Primero que compren (CR), segundo que no abandonen el carrito, y tercero que gasten más (AOV)

In short: First, increase conversion (CR); second, lower cart abandonment; and third, raise the average spend (AOV).

Others Metrics

4. CTA & Above the Fold

Son tus herramientas tácticas visuales en Figma. El contraste del botón y la ubicación en mobile (above the fold) son los elementos de diseño que usás para mover los números de arriba.

5. Bounce Rate (Tasa de Rebote)

Te dice si la Landing Page o la Home están comunicando bien la propuesta de valor. Es clave, pero muchas veces depende también de si el equipo de marketing está trayendo al público correcto a la web.

6. Revenue Growth (Crecimiento de Ingresos)

Es el objetivo final del dueño de la empresa, pero para vos es una consecuencia indirecta. Vos no controlás el precio de los productos ni el stock. Vos mejorás la experiencia de usuario (CR y AOV), y eso genera Revenue Growth. En la entrevista la usás para demostrar que te importa el negocio, pero no es una métrica de diseño pura.

7. LTV o CLV (Customer Lifetime Value - Valor de Vida del Cliente)

Mide cuánta plata gasta un cliente a lo largo de meses o años. Aunque un buen diseño ayuda a que la experiencia sea memorable y el cliente vuelva, la recompra depende un 80% de la calidad del producto, la atención al cliente, el envío y el email marketing. Está fuera de tu control directo en el día a día del diseño web.

8. CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost - Costo de Adquisición)

Esto es 100% terreno del equipo de Marketing Digital (los que arman las campañas en Google Ads, Meta, Instagram). Ellos miden cuánta plata queman en publicidad para traer un clic. A vos te importa saberlo porque si el tráfico es caro, tu Landing Page tiene que ser perfecta para no desperdiciar ese presupuesto, pero vos no manejás el costo del anuncio.

9. Below the Fold

Es simplemente un concepto de ubicación física en la pantalla. Es el opuesto de Above the fold. Es relevante para saber dónde ubicar la información secundaria (especificaciones técnicas, reviews), pero operativamente se maneja en conjunto con el punto 4.

10. Upselling / Cross-selling (Prod Complementario o Relacionado)

No son métricas, son estrategias comerciales. Son las técnicas que usás en la interfaz.

Cross-selling: Ofrecer un producto complementario (ej: llevarte medias cuando comprás zapatillas).

Upselling: Ofrecer una versión superior o más cara del mismo producto (ej: pasar de la campera base a la premium impermeable).

El resultado de ambas técnicas se mide directamente con el AOV.
