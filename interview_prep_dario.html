I also use Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator when I need to edit assets or support production work.

Finally, I like to use AI tools to speed up repetitive tasks in my workflow. This allows me to focus more time on the design decisions that actually matter for the user experience.

Part 3 — Your Process

I start with understanding the business goal and the user's journey — where they come in, what they need to find, and what might be stopping them from converting. Then I work through wireframes to get the structure right before going into final design.

I pay a lot of attention to mobile experience because that's where most of the traffic is. At Blackpepper I worked alongside a CRO team which taught me to think about design in terms of data — heatmaps, drop-off points, conversion metrics — not just aesthetics.

CRO for me starts with understanding where users are dropping off and why. At Blackpepper I worked with a dedicated CRO team — they brought the data and I translated it into design changes. Things like clearer CTAs, better product information hierarchy, reducing friction in the buying journey.

The key is to treat every design decision as a hypothesis — you implement it, measure it, and iterate based on what the data tells you.

I look at where users are dropping off, what they're clicking, and what they're ignoring. At Blackpepper I worked with the CRO team who brought data to the table — heatmaps, session recordings, conversion metrics — and my job was to translate those insights into design changes.

It taught me that good design is never finished — you keep learning from how real people use it.

I start by asking a lot of questions — to the client, to the team, to anyone who understands the space. Then I look at what already exists — competitors, best practices, similar products — not to copy but to understand the landscape.

I try to get in front of real users as quickly as possible, even informally, because assumptions are expensive. And I stay honest about what I don't know — it's much better to ask than to design based on the wrong understanding.

Usually they're more aligned than they seem — a user who can find what they need quickly and feels confident is more likely to convert, which is good for the business. The tension usually appears when a business wants to push something that isn't what the user needs in that moment.

In those cases I try to bring data to the conversation and find a solution that serves both. Design is always a negotiation between different needs, and the best outcomes come from keeping everyone focused on the shared goal.

I'd start by trying to understand if it's a design problem or something else — a technical issue, a change in traffic source, a seasonal pattern. Then I'd look at the data — where exactly is the drop happening, at which step, on which device.

I'd combine that with session recordings and heatmaps to see what users are actually doing. Then I'd form a hypothesis, design a change, and test it. The worst thing you can do is jump straight to a solution before you understand the problem.

It starts with the brief — understanding the goal, the audience, and any technical requirements. I prefer to ask questions early and clear any doubts before I start, because it saves time later.

Then a quick concept to align direction, then the final design. Once approved I prepare the exports — correct sizes, WebP or PNG, compression — and I stay available during implementation to answer developer questions.

For responsiveness I always design mobile-first — not as an afterthought. I test across breakpoints and work closely with developers during implementation. For accessibility I think about contrast, text size, and touch targets from the start.

For brand consistency I work from the guidelines and Figma libraries, and I flag early if something doesn't align rather than fixing it at the end.

Part 4 — Teamwork

I always try to make the team's job as easy as possible. For me, the key is having a clear workflow and open communication from day one.

With developers, I make sure to deliver clean files, organised layers, clear component names, and well-documented specs. I have a basic understanding of HTML and CSS, which helps me design things that are realistic to build. If I ever have a doubt about what is technically possible, I prefer to ask first.

With the marketing team, I focus on aligning with them and understanding the brief completely from the start. I make sure to deliver all visual assets in the right formats and on time to keep brand consistency across all channels.

With Project Managers, I focus on strategic alignment. I make sure to understand the business requirements and timelines so I can prioritise my tasks. If I see any doubt with deadlines or a risk in delivery, I communicate early to find solutions together.

I prioritise projects based on the deadline and business impact. At Blackpepper and Tourism Waitaki I always managed multiple projects at the same time — with different brands, formats, and stages. We used ClickUp to manage tasks, review briefs, and track the whole process.

For me, the key is having a clear system — organised files and clean naming — so I don't lose time on things that should be quick. And I'm very honest when a project needs more time. I prefer to speak up early rather than rushing and delivering a design below quality standards.

I always connect my decisions back to the goal — whether that's conversion, usability, or brand. I don't say "I chose this because it looks good." I say "I chose this because users were dropping off here and this change reduces the friction."

When I have data I use it. When I don't I explain the reasoning clearly. And I'm open to feedback — sometimes a stakeholder sees something I missed. The key is keeping the conversation focused on what we're trying to achieve, not personal preferences.

I listen first and try to understand where it's coming from — sometimes there's context I don't have. If I still disagree I explain my reasoning clearly, connect it back to the goal, and suggest we test it if possible.

I don't dig in just because it's my design. But I also don't change things just to make someone happy if I think it's going to hurt the result. The work has to come first.

At Blackpepper we had a project where the client's marketing team and their internal dev team had opposite opinions about a design direction. Marketing wanted something bold and visually rich, the devs were concerned about performance and implementation complexity.

I brought both sides together in a Figma presentation where I showed two versions — one that achieved the visual impact marketing wanted but was built on a lighter, more performant structure. That gave both sides something to say yes to. The key was making sure neither team felt like they lost.

At Tourism Waitaki we had a website launch that got delayed because the content wasn't ready when the design was. I had finished all the UI work on time but we couldn't launch because key pages were still being written.

It taught me to always clarify content dependencies at the start of a project — design and content can't be treated as separate tracks. Now I always ask early what content exists, what needs to be created, and who's responsible for it.

At Blackpepper there was a project where the brief asked for a design direction I thought was going to hurt conversion — too much visual noise on the product page. I wasn't the decision maker but I put together a short comparison in Figma showing the proposed direction against a cleaner alternative, with a note explaining the reasoning from a conversion perspective.

The team went with the cleaner version. I think it worked because I didn't push my opinion — I showed the reasoning and let the work speak.

I really enjoyed it. The pace is fast and you're always working across different brands and challenges, which keeps you sharp. The key is being organised and communicating well — with the design team, with account managers, and with developers.

At Blackpepper I worked in a small design team of three, which meant I had a lot of ownership over my work and had to be across everything from concept to handoff.

I work best when there's a clear goal but freedom in how to get there. I like environments where design is taken seriously as a business tool — not just decoration — and where there's a close relationship between design and development.

I also work well under pressure and in fast-paced environments, as long as there's good communication. At Blackpepper that was the case and I thrived in it.
